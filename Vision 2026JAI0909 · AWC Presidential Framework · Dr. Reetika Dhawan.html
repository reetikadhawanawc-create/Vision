AWC Mission
"Transforming lives through education and partnerships to create thriving communities."
Dear AWC Family,

It is with deep gratitude, humility, and optimism that I begin this journey as President of Arizona Western College.

For decades, Arizona Western College has served as a gateway to opportunity for students and families across Yuma and La Paz Counties. As a rural Hispanic-Serving Institution rooted in community, AWC has transformed lives through access, workforce education, transfer opportunity, and unwavering commitment to student success. Today, we stand at another defining moment.

Across the nation, community colleges are being called to lead in new ways, preparing students for artificial intelligence, healthcare transformation, advanced manufacturing, cybersecurity, quantum technologies, entrepreneurship, and the future of work itself. Institutions are also being asked to address affordability, housing, belonging, workforce shortages, and economic mobility in increasingly complex environments. Arizona Western College is uniquely positioned to lead this moment.

This 100-Day Presidential Framework is not simply a plan. It is an invitation to listen deeply, think boldly, embrace innovation responsibly, and shape a future worthy of our students' dreams and our region's aspirations. Over the coming months, I will spend time across all our campuses and communities: Yuma, San Luis, Somerton, Parker, Quartzsite, and beyond, hearing directly from students, faculty, staff, employers, tribal leaders, elected officials, alumni, and community members.

Together, We Will Explore These Essential Questions
  • What should the future of higher education look like in rural Arizona?
  • How should AWC prepare students for careers that may not yet exist?
  • What bachelor's degree pathways are most needed in our region?
  • How do we support faculty innovation while preserving academic excellence?
  • How can artificial intelligence expand opportunity rather than widen equity gaps?
  • What does true belonging look like for every student and employee at AWC?
  • How can AWC become Arizona's leading rural and border innovation college?

The answers will shape Vision 2026: a shared framework grounded in student success, workforce mobility, transfer excellence, innovation, fiscal sustainability, and community impact. This is our moment to move forward with purpose, clarity, and collaboration.

Dr. Reetika Dhawan

Context & Opportunity

Why This Moment Matters

Higher education is changing rapidly. Community colleges now sit at the center of workforce transformation, AI integration, regional economic development, transfer and bachelor's pathways, and student basic needs support. Arizona Western College has an opportunity not simply to respond to change, but to lead it.

National Landscape

  • Enrollment rebounding toward flexible, affordable, career-connected options

AI Skills for All

AACC's national "AI Skills for All" initiative, in partnership with Microsoft, is building AI literacy across community college systems. AWC has the opportunity to join or replicate this community of practice, positioning itself as an AI-integrated institution from presidential day one.

Basic Needs & Housing

28% of community colleges now provide student housing, which is a growing imperative. 39% of students report food insecurity; 12% report homelessness. These barriers are the #1 completion threat. AWC's rural geography makes this especially critical for Yuma, Parker, and border students.

Baccalaureate Expansion

Community colleges awarded 706,483 baccalaureate degrees nationally, up 18% in five years. Arizona allows applied baccalaureate programs. AWC can explore bachelor's completion pathways that keep rural students local and reduce transfer out-migration.

Working Learners

58% of community college students work while enrolled; average student age is 28. This means flexible scheduling, stackable credentials, evening/weekend/online options, and wrap-around support services are not optional; they are mission-critical at AWC.

Workforce & Transfer Alignment

Employers rate problem-solving, digital fluency, and communication as top graduate competencies. 48% of community college students intend to transfer to a 4-year institution. AWC's partnerships with UA, ASU, and NAU directly serve this national imperative.

Federal Funding & HSI Grants

Billions in federal funds flow through community colleges annually. Competitive grant programs supporting Hispanic-Serving Institutions, STEM education, rural development, and student support services remain active funding priorities. AWC's identity as an HSI in a rural border community creates significant federal and philanthropic grant eligibility.

AWC's Strategic Advantage in This National Landscape

AWC is not just responding to national trends; it is positioned to lead them. As a Hispanic-Serving Institution in a rural border region with growing workforce needs, AWC exemplifies the exact institutions AACC, Congress, and national foundations are investing in. Every trend above is an argument for AWC's relevance, a grant narrative waiting to be written, and a reason for partners to say yes.

Our Guiding Framework

Three Pillars of Presidential Leadership

Belonging
Belonging does not happen by chance; it is created through intention, care, and relationship. We will foster a culture where students, faculty, staff, and community partners feel valued, respected, heard, and empowered to thrive. AWC's culture must reflect compassion, accountability, inclusiveness, and mutual respect. Every student will see themselves reflected in opportunity; every employee will feel connected to our mission.
Collaboration
Our greatest progress happens when we work together. Through partnerships with students, faculty, universities, employers, healthcare systems, tribal communities, regional leaders, and economic development organizations, we will co-create innovative solutions for our communities. Collaboration will not simply be encouraged; it will become foundational to how we lead, plan, and serve at Arizona Western College.
Communication
Transparent communication builds trust. We will communicate openly about priorities, opportunities, challenges, budget realities, institutional decisions, and shared progress. We will celebrate successes, acknowledge challenges honestly, and ensure our AWC community remains informed and engaged throughout this transition and beyond. Trust is built one transparent conversation at a time.
Vision 2026 Strategic Themes

Eight Priorities Shaping AWC's Future

Student Success & Belonging

Strengthening retention, persistence, completion, transfer, and workforce success, with intentional focus on first-generation, rural, adult, multilingual, and historically underserved students. Belonging is reflected in advising, instruction, support services, and community connection.

Workforce & Economic Mobility

Aligning educational pathways with healthcare, advanced manufacturing, agtech, logistics, cybersecurity, renewable energy, and emerging technologies. Students leave AWC not only with credentials, but with clear pathways to economic mobility, career advancement, and lifelong learning.

Bachelor's Degree Pathways & Transfer Excellence

Exploring expanded educational pathways and workforce-aligned bachelor's opportunities. Strengthening transfer partnerships with ASU, NAU, U of A, and additional university partners to create seamless student pathways that match community need and workforce demand.

Artificial Intelligence & Future Workforce

Proactively preparing students, faculty, and the institution for AI-enhanced learning: AI literacy initiatives, responsible AI policy, faculty professional development, microcredentials, and AI applications across healthcare, agriculture, education, logistics, and manufacturing.

Rural & Border Innovation

Positioning AWC as a national leader through rural healthcare workforce development, agriculture technology, water and sustainability innovation, logistics and international trade, bilingual workforce development, cross-border economic partnerships, and community entrepreneurship.

Faculty Excellence & Support

Investing in instructional excellence, professional development, innovation in teaching and learning, and faculty support systems. Shared governance and academic expertise remain foundational to institutional decision-making as higher education evolves rapidly.

Student Housing & Basic Needs

Engaging students and communities in conversations around housing, transportation, food insecurity, childcare, mental health, and wraparound services. Addressing basic needs is not separate from student success; it is central to it.

Fiscal Sustainability & Strategic Grants

Pursuing transformational grants, strategic partnerships, and innovative funding aligned with institutional priorities, while maintaining transparency around budget realities, enrollment trends, operational efficiencies, and long-term sustainability for future generations.

Goal 1 · Listening Tour

Launch a Comprehensive AWC Presidential Listening Tour

The AWC community has stories, aspirations, and concerns that must be deeply heard before any new direction is set. Visit every campus and site: Yuma, San Luis, Somerton, Parker, Quartzsite, and beyond.

Key Actions
  • Schedule 1:1 meetings with all vice presidents, deans, and direct cabinet members within the first two weeks
  • Host open listening sessions for AWC faculty, staff, and students across all campuses and sites
  • Meet with AWC Student Government, Faculty Senate, Classified Staff Council, and Professional Staff leadership
  • Attend student-organized events and AWC campus traditions to understand the lived institutional culture
  • Hold dedicated listening sessions with dual enrollment partners, high school principals, and K–12 district leaders
  • Engage directly with students on housing, food insecurity, childcare, transportation, and basic needs barriers
  • Initiate conversations on AI readiness with faculty and workforce partners across all divisions
Measurable Outcome100% of direct reports met individually; minimum 6 listening sessions held (at least 2 in La Paz County); bachelor's degree, AI, and housing conversations initiated; feedback themes documented and shared publicly

Goal 2 · Institutional Review

Conduct a Deep Review of AWC's Institutional Health

Develop a clear, data-grounded picture of where AWC stands: academically, financially, and operationally. Review grants, partnerships, and accreditation status (HLC) before making commitments or shaping direction.

Key Actions
  • Review AWC's current strategic plan, HLC accreditation status, and most recent self-evaluation report
  • Analyze 5-year enrollment trends, student completion/transfer rates, and demographic data across AWC's service area
  • Review institutional budget, reserve funds, debt obligations, and long-term financial sustainability projections
  • Read recent District Governing Board meeting minutes and all unresolved action items
  • Review employee engagement surveys, CCSSE/Noel-Levitz data, and recent program reviews
  • Conduct a comprehensive review of existing grants, partnerships, and external funding pipeline
  • Assess faculty support resources: instructional technology, professional development budgets, classroom infrastructure
Measurable OutcomePresidential briefing document completed; KPI dashboard established; grants and partnership inventory complete; budget snapshot presented to the District Governing Board

Goal 1 · Community Synthesis

Publish "What I've Heard at AWC" & Launch Vision 2026 Forums

Transparency builds trust. After deep listening, distill the themes into an honest summary the community can act on; then invite the community into Vision 2026 co-creation forums across all AWC sites and counties.

Key Actions

Goal 2 · Cabinet & Governance

Assess the Cabinet, Draft President's Council & Strengthen Governance

The President's Cabinet and the new President's Council become the collaborative engine for Vision 2026 implementation. Build cohesion and clarity now.

Key Actions
  • Hold individual 60-minute "deep-dive" sessions with each cabinet member on division goals, resource needs, and gaps
  • Co-create shared leadership principles and a decision-making charter with the cabinet
  • Draft the AWC President's Council structure: cross-divisional body representing faculty, staff, students, and external partners
  • Assess organizational structure for alignment with AWC's mission and Vision 2026 strategic direction
  • Strengthen shared governance by clarifying Faculty Senate, Staff Council, and Student Government roles
  • Launch a Student Basic Needs & Housing Task Force with cross-divisional representation
